The Legends and Myths of the Xian Great Mosque

Home / Travel Blog / Blog Details

There’s a place in the heart of Xian, just a stone's throw from the roaring drums and neon signs of the Muslim Quarter, where time doesn’t just slow down—it folds in on itself. This is the Xian Great Mosque (西安大清真寺, Xī'ān Dà Qīngzhēnsì), one of the oldest, largest, and most breathtakingly serene Islamic mosques in all of China. But to call it merely a mosque is to tell only half the story. It is a living archive, a testament to the ancient Silk Road, and a vessel for some of the most captivating legends and myths that bridge the celestial and the earthly. For the modern traveler, it's not just a stop on the itinerary; it's an immersion into a narrative a thousand years in the making.

A Sanctuary Forged on the Silk Road

The story of the Xian Great Mosque is inextricably linked to the greatest trade network in human history: the Silk Road. Xian, known then as Chang'an, was the glittering eastern terminus of this sprawling web of routes. It was a metropolis of unparalleled wealth, culture, and religious diversity. Along with bolts of silk, spices, and precious stones, ideas traveled. And in the 7th century, during the golden age of the Tang Dynasty, Islam arrived in China.

The Legend of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas

History and myth intertwine at the mosque's very foundation. The most pervasive legend attributes its establishment to Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas, a maternal uncle of the Prophet Muhammad himself. As the story goes, Sa'd was sent as an emissary to the Tang Emperor Gaozong around 651 AD. Welcomed with open arms, he and his delegation were granted permission to spread their faith and build a place of worship. While historical records outside of folklore are debated, this powerful origin story is central to the mosque's identity. It symbolizes a divine connection and an immediate, respectful dialogue between two great civilizations. It speaks of a welcome that was never revoked, setting the stage for the Hui Muslim community's deep roots in Chinese soil.

The Myth of the Phoenix and the Miraculous Orientation

Every visitor notices something peculiar. Traditional mosques around the world are oriented towards Mecca, so worshippers know which direction to pray (the qibla). In China, that would mean facing roughly west-southwest. But the layout of the Xian Great Mosque feels distinctly, and confusingly, Chinese—a series of pavilions and courtyards on an east-west axis. How did the builders solve this?

A beautiful myth offers an explanation. It is said that the community prayed for divine guidance on the construction. In answer, a phoenix, a sacred symbol in Chinese mythology representing harmony and prosperity, descended from the heavens. It landed on the chosen site, and with a graceful sweep of its wing, it indicated the precise alignment for the prayer hall—a perfect orientation towards Mecca within a perfectly Chinese complex. This legend elegantly resolves the architectural marvel, framing it not as a compromise but as a heavenly miracle, a fusion of faiths and cultures made sacred by a celestial creature respected by all.

Architectural Myths: Dragons Serving the Divine

Walking through the mosque's five successive courtyards is like moving through a living storybook. The architecture is a harmonious blend of Islamic function and Ming and Qing Dynasty artistry. This unique synthesis gives rise to some of the most visually striking features and the myths that surround them.

The Dragon Pillars of the Prayer Hall

Inside the vast prayer hall, which can hold over a thousand worshippers, towering wooden pillars support the massive roof. carved into these pillars are intricate coiling dragons. In traditional Chinese culture, the dragon is a potent symbol of imperial power and celestial strength. To see them in a mosque seems, to some, paradoxical. But the local legend reframes their purpose. It is said these are not imperial dragons but celestial guardians converted to a new purpose. They were tasked by a higher power to shed their imperial vanity and instead use their strength to serve the divine, forever holding aloft the roof that shelters the faithful in prayer. They symbolize submission to Allah, a powerful metaphor for the integration of a community into the fabric of Chinese society while maintaining its unique faith.

The Moon Pavilion and the Immortal's Plum Blossoms

One of the most photographed structures is the exquisite "Introspection Minaret" (省心楼, Shěngxīn Lóu), often called the Moon Pavilion for its beauty. This three-story, octagonal tower is used for the call to prayer. A charming myth surrounds the ancient plum tree in its courtyard. It's said that the tree was planted by an immortal who visited the mosque during its construction. He proclaimed that as long as the tree bloomed, the mosque would stand protected and the community would thrive. To this day, the blooming of the plum blossoms each year is seen as a blessing and a reaffirmation of this ancient promise, making it a huge draw for photographers and Instagrammers seeking that perfect shot of natural beauty against ancient architecture.

The Modern Traveler's Pilgrimage: More Than Just a Visit

For today's traveler, the Xian Great Mosque is a top-tier cultural hotspot, a must-visit nestled within the already-unmissable Muslim Quarter. But understanding its legends transforms the experience from sightseeing to time traveling.

A Culinary Adventure Rooted in History

The visit is never complete without diving into the Muslim Quarter's food scene. This isn't just a food market; it's a direct culinary legacy of the Silk Road. The sizzling yangrou chuan (mutton skewers), the steaming bowls of paomo (crumbled flatbread in mutton stew), the sweet persimmon cakes—these recipes are heirlooms, passed down through generations of Hui families. The legend goes that the spices used in the rub for the famous skewers were first bartered for by Arab traders along the Silk Road and refined over centuries in Xian's kitchens. Every bite is a taste of history. Food bloggers and travel vloggers can spend an entire day here, tracing the origins of each dish back to the mosque and the communities it nurtured.

The Seekers of Blessings and the "Whispering Wall"

A current travel trend involves seekers—travelers looking for meaningful, spiritual experiences rather than just souvenirs. The mosque caters perfectly to this. Near the back of the complex, a section of an old wall is known among locals as the "Whispering Wall." The myth states that if you whisper a sincere prayer or a heartfelt wish into the crevices of its ancient stones, the spirits of the learned imams and pious travelers who have passed through will carry it heavenward. Whether one believes it or not, participating in this ritual connects the modern traveler to the countless souls who have sought solace within these walls for over a millennium. It’s a powerful, personal moment that dominates travel diaries and blogs.

Capturing the Light: A Photographer's Legend

Photographers, both amateur and professional, whisper their own legend about the "Golden Hour" at the mosque. It's said that the light during the late afternoon is not merely sunlight filtering through the ancient cypress trees. The myth suggests it is the accumulated glow of all the prayers offered over the centuries, made visible for a brief moment each day. This "holy light" is believed to bless any photograph taken within it. This makes the hours before dusk the most sought-after time for visitation, with tripods lining the courtyards as visitors try to capture the mosque's ethereal beauty and, perhaps, a little bit of its magic for themselves.

The Xian Great Mosque is far more than a historical relic. It is a vibrant, active place of worship, a cultural landmark, and a repository of stories that give depth and meaning to every arched gateway, every carved stone, and every silent courtyard. Its legends of heavenly phoenixes, guardian dragons, and whispering walls provide a layer of enchantment that elevates a tourist's visit into a traveler's tale. It stands as a permanent, beautiful argument for harmony, a place where two great worlds met and, instead of clashing, built something wonderous together.
